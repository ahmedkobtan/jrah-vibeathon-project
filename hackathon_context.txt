Routes and purpose
Routes are the sequences of stops a community health worker (CHW) will follow during a shift to visit clients, deliver services, pick up supplies, or run errands. Good routing minimizes travel time and distance, respects time windows (appointments), and maximizes number of completed tasks given CHW capacity and constraints. For the MVP, routes are used to present an optimized, actionable day plan the CHW can accept or modify.

What TSP means
TSP = Traveling Salesman Problem. It asks: given a list of locations and distances between them, what order should you visit every location exactly once and return to the start, minimizing total distance (or time). TSP is a classical combinatorial optimization problem; exact solutions are expensive for large numbers of stops but trivial for small numbers typical of CHW daily routes.

Why TSP (or similar) matters for CHWs
Reduces travel time and fuel cost so CHWs see more clients per day.

Lowers wait times for clients and improves reliability of same‑day services.

Makes schedules predictable and simpler for volunteers/drivers.

Provides a clear, demonstrable benefit in the demo (show travel distance/time before vs after optimization).

Practical routing problem formulation for CHW MVP
Inputs (seeded CSV or API):

Stops: id;lat;lon;service-duration-min;time-window-start;time-window-end;priority.

CHW: start-location-lat;start-location-lon;shift-start;shift-end;max-visits.

Travel matrix: estimated travel time or distance between locations (use driving API or Haversine approximation).

Objective: minimize total travel time (or distance) while maximizing completed high‑priority visits and respecting time windows and CHW shift length.

Constraints: service durations, time windows, CHW capacity, optional return-to-base.

Algorithms and heuristics for a 2‑person weekend MVP
For small daily routes (≤ 10 stops) use simple, fast methods:

Greedy nearest neighbor (fast, decent).

2‑opt local improvements on top of greedy (removes obvious detours).

Time‑window aware insertion heuristic (insert next best feasible stop by earliest deadline).

For slightly larger or higher quality:

Use OR‑Tools vehicle routing solver (supports time windows, capacities, multiple vehicles) — easy to run and integrates with Python.

Use simulated annealing or genetic algorithm libraries if you want novelty.

For demo simplicity: implement Nearest Neighbor + 2‑opt and show a visual before/after route on a map.

Reinforcement learning for CHW routing — short verdict
Reinforcement learning (RL) can help CHW routing, especially for dynamic, stochastic, or large‑scale problems (online requests, uncertain travel times, multi‑worker coordination). For small, static daily routes with tight time windows, classic combinatorial solvers (OR‑Tools, greedy+2‑opt) are faster, simpler, and more predictable — RL adds value when the environment is complex, nonstationary, or when you want an adaptive policy that learns tradeoffs from real experience
CMU School of Computer Science
.

When RL makes sense for CHW routing
Requests arrive online during a shift (new visits, cancellations, urgent add‑ons).

Travel times or service durations are highly stochastic or dependent on time of day.

You must jointly optimize routing plus nonrouting controls (when to accept new referrals, which CHW to dispatch, dynamic prioritization).

You want a learned policy that balances long‑term objectives (max visits per day, patient equity, burn‑out minimization) rather than a single‑day optimal route
CMU School of Computer Science
.

You need fast, repeated decisions at scale (many CHWs and many stochastic requests) where recomputing exact optimization each time is too slow
IEEE Xplore
+1
.

(If your problem is small, deterministic, and offline — e.g., 5–8 stops per CHW with fixed time windows — use deterministic solvers for the Vibeathon demo.)

How to formulate the CHW routing problem as an RL problem
Environment

Simulated world that emits referral requests, travel times between coordinates (possibly stochastic), and CHW states (location, remaining shift time, capacity).

Includes exogenous events: cancellations, new high‑priority tasks, volunteer availability changes.

State

CHW state: current location, clock time, remaining capacity.

Queue: list of pending stops with (lat, lon, service_duration, priority, time_window).

Context: forecast travel times, traffic features, CHW fatigue estimate, historical no‑show probabilities.

Actions

Assign next stop (choose one of pending tasks), or wait/return‑to‑base, or request backup.

At higher level: dispatch decisions (which CHW to assign a new request).

Optionally: adjust speed/route or reschedule existing appointments.

Transition dynamics

Deterministic update of location/time after taking action plus stochastic outcomes (actual travel time, whether patient is present, new arrivals).

Reward design (critical)

Positive: completed visit (weighted by priority), on‑time arrivals, client satisfaction proxies.

Negative: travel time, idle time, overtime, missed high‑priority requests, CHW fatigue penalty.

Use shaped rewards: short‑term (per‑step) + episode terminal bonus for coverage equity or overall throughput.

Episode design

One CHW’s shift = one episode; multi‑CHW episodes modeled with centralized or decentralized policies.

Candidate RL approaches
Model‑free policy optimization (PPO, A2C, TRPO): stable, works with continuous/discrete actions, good for complex reward shaping and large state spaces.

Value‑based (DQN variants): suitable for discrete action spaces when action set is small (e.g., choose next stop among a handful).

Multi‑agent RL (MADDPG, QMIX): for coordinating multiple CHWs where centralized training / decentralized execution is desired.
